## Why your brain forgets, and why that is useful

Spaced repetition is the most-cited and least-used study method in cognitive science. Almost every student has heard of it. Almost none use it consistently. The gap is not motivation. It is that most students do not understand what spacing actually does, so they cannot make it fit their real course load.

Here is the core idea. Your brain forgets new information on a predictable curve. The first 24 hours are brutal — without intervention, you lose roughly half of what you encoded in a lecture. After a week, you might be at 20 percent. The forgetting curve is real, it is steep, and you cannot will it away.

Spaced repetition exploits the curve. Every time you successfully retrieve a piece of information just as you were about to forget it, the next forgetting curve is shallower. Five timed retrievals, properly spaced, can take a fact from "gone in two days" to "still there in two years." Cramming cannot do this. Re-reading cannot do this. Only retrieval at the right interval can.

## The mechanism: retrieval at the edge of forgetting

The optimal moment to review something is right when you are about to forget it. Too early and the review is wasted because the memory is still vivid. Too late and you have to re-learn from scratch.

This is why spacing works and cramming does not. Cramming hits the same material five times in a row, all when the memory is already strong, so no retrieval is happening. Spaced repetition hits the same material five times across weeks, each at the moment your brain is doing real work to pull it back. The work is the encoding.

You do not have to time this perfectly. The intervals are forgiving. A schedule of 1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 3 weeks, before the exam will dramatically outperform any cram session of equal total hours.

## A simple spacing schedule that works in real courses

You do not need an algorithm to start. Try this schedule for any new topic:

- **Day 0:** Learn the material (lecture, reading, problem set).
- **Day 1:** First retrieval. Closed-book brain dump, flashcards, or a few practice problems on the topic.
- **Day 3 or 4:** Second retrieval. Same format, briefer.
- **Day 10:** Third retrieval. Mixed with other topics if possible.
- **Day 25 to 30:** Fourth retrieval. By now, this takes very little time per topic.
- **Before the exam:** Final pass, with extra time on whatever is weak.

This is roughly five touches across a month for each piece of material. The total time investment per topic is small. The retention difference vs. a single cram session is enormous.

## Where Anki helps

Anki is the most popular spaced repetition software, and for good reason. It implements an algorithm (SM-2 or similar) that schedules each card individually based on how easy you found it last time. Easy cards get pushed further out, hard cards come back sooner.

Anki is excellent for:

- **Discrete factual material.** Vocabulary in any language. Anatomy. Drug mechanisms. Historical dates. Biological pathways. Definitions in any field.
- **Med school, law school, and pre-med biology.** The card-and-cue format maps cleanly to the recall requirements of these fields.
- **Long-term retention.** If you need to remember it next year, not just for the exam, Anki is hard to beat.

The students who use Anki well treat it as a daily habit, 10 to 30 minutes a day, and never let the queue blow up.

## Where Anki fails

Anki is bad at:

- **Synthesis and reasoning.** It cannot quiz you on "given this set of facts, derive this conclusion." It can only quiz you on facts.
- **Problem-solving courses.** Math, physics, engineering. The retrieval is not "what is the formula" — it is "can I solve this kind of problem," and that requires worked examples and practice sets, not flashcards.
- **Anything where the card-writing is harder than the studying.** If you spend 90 minutes making cards and 10 minutes reviewing them, you optimized the wrong end.
- **The trap of "I'll learn it from the cards."** Cards are for retrieval of what you already learned. They do not teach you the underlying material.

For most college courses, the right move is hybrid: use spaced retrieval as the *schedule*, and use whatever format fits the material (flashcards, problem sets, brain dumps, past exams) as the *retrieval task*.

## Spaced repetition without software

You do not need an app. A working low-tech version:

- A physical box with index cards in five compartments.
- Compartment 1 is daily. Compartment 2 is every 3 days. Compartment 3 weekly. Compartment 4 every two weeks. Compartment 5 monthly.
- A card you get right moves up a compartment. A card you miss goes back to compartment 1.

This is the Leitner system. It is what Anki digitized. It works exactly as well for most students, with no software friction.

## The cognitive bias that wrecks spaced repetition

The fluency illusion: when you re-read material and recognize it, you feel like you know it. You do not. Recognition is not retrieval.

Spaced repetition only works when the retrieval is *actually* happening. If you flip the card and your brain "kind of knew it" but you check the answer before truly producing it, the spacing benefit collapses.

The rule: produce the answer in full, out loud or in writing, before flipping the card. Effortful retrieval is what builds the memory. Easy retrieval that feels nice produces almost nothing.

## Interleaving makes spacing better

Spacing tells you *when* to retrieve. Interleaving tells you *how* to mix the retrievals.

If you sit down for a spaced repetition session and do 30 minutes of organic chemistry, then 30 minutes of physiology, then 30 minutes of biochem, the spacing within each topic is wasted because you blocked them. Interleave: chemistry card, physiology card, biochem card, chemistry card, physiology card. Your accuracy during practice drops. Your exam performance climbs.

This works because the exam will not group questions by topic, and the discriminating between topics is part of what is being tested.

## The realistic problem: doing this for 5 classes at once

A single course with spaced repetition is manageable. Five courses, each with weekly new material, each needing four scheduled reviews on different days, is a scheduling nightmare. Doing this on paper for a full course load is what makes most students quit by week three.

This is the actual reason students do not use spaced repetition, even though they know it works. It is not laziness. It is that the planning load is genuinely large.
