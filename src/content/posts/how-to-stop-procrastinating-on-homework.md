## Procrastination is not a laziness problem

The most useful sentence in the entire procrastination literature: procrastination is an emotion regulation problem, not a time management problem.

You are not procrastinating because you do not know the homework is due. You know. You are procrastinating because starting the task triggers something uncomfortable — boredom, anxiety about doing it badly, resentment, overwhelm — and avoiding the task makes the discomfort go away in the short term. The relief is the reward. The pattern reinforces itself.

This is why "just do it" advice does not work. You are not trying harder. You are trying to avoid a feeling. Every solution below addresses the feeling first and the work second.

## The two-minute starting rule

The hardest part of any task is the first two minutes. The resistance peaks right before you begin, and almost always collapses once you have started.

The commitment is not "I will do my homework." It is "I will do two minutes of my homework." Not preparation, not setting up the desk, not opening the file. Two minutes of the actual task: writing the first sentence, doing the first problem, reading the first page.

This works for a specific reason: the resistance you feel is to the *idea* of the task, which is huge and undefined in your head. Two minutes of contact replaces the imagined task with the real task, which is almost always smaller and more concrete than the imagined version.

You will, almost every time, keep going past two minutes. The two minutes is the entry cost. The momentum is free.

## Implementation intentions beat resolutions

"I will study tonight" is not a plan. It is a hope. The research is consistent: vague intentions almost never produce action. Specific implementation intentions do.

The format that works:

> When [specific trigger], I will [specific action] at [specific location].

Examples:
- "When I finish dinner at 7pm, I will sit at my desk and start my physics problem set."
- "When I get home from class on Tuesday, I will open my laptop on the kitchen table and write the first paragraph of my essay."

The trigger is what your brain attaches the new behavior to. Without a trigger, the action depends on motivation, which is unreliable. With a trigger, the action becomes automatic enough to bypass the resistance.

This is the single most-studied intervention in behavior change, and it produces effect sizes that dwarf almost everything else. Use it.

## Make the task smaller than feels reasonable

The size of the task in your head is almost always larger than the actual task. "Write the essay" feels enormous. "Write the topic sentence of paragraph two" is small enough that resistance collapses.

The rule: if you are procrastinating, the next action is too big. Shrink it until it is embarrassingly small. The first move on an essay is not "write the essay" or even "write a draft." It is "open the document and write the first sentence." That is the next action.

This is not infantilizing. It is matching the size of the commitment to your current resistance. Once you have started, the next next-action appears naturally.

## Identify what feeling you are actually avoiding

The reason "just start" is hard is that under the procrastination is a specific feeling, and naming it changes its grip on you.

Common ones:

- **Fear of doing the work badly.** Especially for graded work. Avoidance protects the fantasy that you would do well if you tried.
- **Boredom.** The task is genuinely tedious and your brain prefers anything else.
- **Resentment.** The professor is unfair, the assignment is pointless, you should not have to do this.
- **Overwhelm.** The task is large and you do not see a path through it.
- **Confusion.** You do not actually understand what is being asked, and starting feels like exposing the confusion.

Each one has a different intervention. Fear of doing badly responds to lowering the standard of the first draft. Boredom responds to constraint (timer, music-free, hard mode for 20 minutes only). Resentment responds to honest acknowledgment, then doing it anyway. Overwhelm responds to shrinking the next action. Confusion responds to office hours or a one-question Slack message to a classmate.

The fastest way out is naming which one it is. "I am avoiding this because I am afraid the draft will be bad" is already half the work.

## Pre-decide everything you can

Every decision you have to make during the work is a chance for the resistance to win. Pre-decide so that when the time comes, there is nothing to negotiate with.

- **The specific time you will start, written on the calendar.**
- **The specific location.**
- **The first 20 minutes' specific task.**
- **What you are doing immediately after a small break (back to the work, not a different task).**

The students who reliably get started are not making fewer mistakes in the moment. They made the decisions earlier, when they were not avoiding anything yet.

## Lower the cost of starting

The friction between you and starting is usually small but real:

- Your laptop is in another room.
- The document is buried in a Drive folder.
- Your desk has yesterday's mess on it.
- You need a charger.

These look trivial. They are the actual reason you do not start. Each one is a five-second opportunity to bail.

The night before: put the laptop on the desk, open the document, clear the surface. The next day's friction is gone before you have a chance to use it.

## The "I'll start when I feel like it" trap

You will not feel like it. Waiting for motivation is the most common cause of academic disasters, and the research is unambiguous: motivation follows action, not the other way around.

The pattern is: you do not feel like starting, so you do not start, so you do not get the small dopamine hit of progress that would have produced motivation. The motivation never arrives because the action never happens.

The inversion: start without motivation. Two minutes of work produces the feeling of being in motion, which produces motivation, which produces the next ten minutes. Action is the cause, not the result.

## When to use deadlines (and when they backfire)

Tight deadlines work in two specific situations: when the work is bounded and concrete (a problem set, a coding assignment) and when you can actually meet them.

Tight deadlines backfire when:

- The work requires creative thinking that does not respond to time pressure.
- You routinely miss them and now your brain treats deadlines as suggestions.
- The deadline is so far away that "I have time" is the avoidance mechanism.

For long-term projects, the better tool is a chain of small interim deadlines, each with a specific deliverable. The 8-page paper due in three weeks becomes: outline by Tuesday, first two pages by Friday, draft by next Friday. The interim deliverables are the deadlines that actually move work.
